Our Past, The Foundation

Humans once, and for the longest time lived in sync with the earth's natural rhythms. We slept long nights in cool, dark, quiet environments. We wore natural cloth designed for movement, and the weather, and could breath deep the unpolluted air. We had access to clean water and drank small amounts often as we were busy all day outside under the sun. We lived in community, collecting, hunting, and cultivating the largest variety of foods possible. We ate small, seasonal meals, and had times of famine and feast. We lived in relative harmony with each other, and the Natural world.

Children breastfeed for years and childbirth naturally spaced to optimize health and growth, which kept the birth rate and population size manageable, and slowly paced the development of civilization. We lived in multigenerational communities, cultivated crops, and raised animals. But we also traveled distances for food and resources. If we survived childhood, accident, and childbirth, chronic disease was rarer and we often lived long, healthy, cognizant lives well into a great old age.

This was lifestyle at the point in history (about 10,000 yrs. ago) that current research is finding that homo-sapiens were at our healthiest.

A Living Diet

Is derived from the largest variety of fresh, natural whole foods. Is minimally processed, cooked, and eaten in such a way as to increase the taste, enjoyment, nutrient absorption, and aids in proper digestion.

Eat - mostly plants, non-starchy vegetables, mushrooms, and a fruit or two daily. Sea food, fish, poultry, eggs and small game (rabbits etc.) and some occasional raw milk dairy - for extra, easy protein. Less often eat starchy roots, grains, legumes and more often, but fewer in amounts, nuts and seeds (your hand size cupped full). Large game animals - "red meat" - eat only a few times a week, or more in hunting or kidding season and through the cold winter months. Minimize honey, syrups and sugars - especially the artifical ones.

This recommendation varies person to person depending on the output, habits, and individuality of the person; usually in the amount eaten, rather than a change of ingredients.

Wild and perennial plants and vegetables have the longest and most reciprocal relationship with the soil-food web, which feeds both us, and the animals we consume. It is this relationship that creates the most nutrient dense and protective foods available. They can be carefully, and ethically wild-crafted, cultivated as perennials in your garden, and are often known as culinary herbs and weeds.

On going agricultural research is improving the bioavailability and nutrient density of our crops - regenerative, permaculture, and adaptive farming etc. use methods and techniques. For example, creating short stressors for the plants during the growing season, which strengthens and improves their storage capacity, taste, and nutrient levels; or by withholding water just before harvest concentrating the flavors and increasing the amount of antioxidants and phytonutrients available. As well, using other techniques: Like careful seed selection from plants well adapted to their environments to create optimal conditions for strong, resistant crops and great tasting produce.
